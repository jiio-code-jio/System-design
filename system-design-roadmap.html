How to read this

Each phase has one primary resource — the one you actually study cover-to-cover — and two or three supplementary ones for the inevitable confusion. Don't try to consume all of them in parallel. That's the most common failure mode.

The phases assume roughly 10 hours per week. Less than that and you'll forget Phase 01 by the time you finish Phase 03. More and you'll halve the timeline. Either way, finish a phase before starting the next — the dependencies are real.

01Foundations

The building blocks

3 weeks/Required vocabulary

Every system design conversation assumes fluent vocabulary. You need to casually distinguish a reverse proxy from an API gateway, explain why a write-through cache fails under partition, and compare consistent hashing to range-based sharding without pausing to think.

This is the language phase. The goal isn't to design anything yet — it's to make the next phase legible.

Load balancers · L4 vs L7 · Reverse proxies · API gateways · Caching strategies · CDN · Message queues · Pub/sub · Replication · Sharding · Consistent hashing · Indexing · REST vs gRPC vs WebSockets · Long polling vs SSE

02Storage

Databases under load

3 weeks/What the DB actually does

"SQL or NoSQL" is the most boring database question ever asked and you'll get it in every interview. The interesting question — and the one that separates juniors from seniors — is which kind of NoSQL, and why. That requires knowing what's happening below the query.

You'll go from "I use Postgres" to understanding why Postgres uses B-trees, why Cassandra uses LSM trees, why MongoDB's sharding is range-based by default, and what trade-off DynamoDB made when it picked partition keys.

B-tree vs LSM tree · Write-ahead logs · MVCC · Index types · Query planning · Partitioning · Replication topologies · Read replicas · Postgres · MySQL · Cassandra · MongoDB · DynamoDB · Redis · ElasticSearch

03The deep end

Distributed systems

6 weeks/The hardest phase. The most important phase.

This phase is what separates engineers who memorize system design from engineers who do it. The vocabulary you learned in Phase 01 is the vocabulary of describing systems. The vocabulary here is the vocabulary of reasoning about them — failure modes, partial outages, consensus, partition tolerance.

Block out six full weeks. Do not rush. DDIA is the only book in this entire roadmap that you should read twice, with a notebook open the second time. Most engineers who try DDIA give up around Chapter 5 — don't be one of them.

CAP theorem · PACELC · ACID · BASE · Linearizability · Eventual consistency · Consensus · Raft · Paxos · Leader election · Two-phase commit · Sagas · Vector clocks · Lamport timestamps · Distributed transactions · Quorums

04Async

Streams & event-driven systems

3 weeks/The async half of modern architecture

Modern systems are not request-response — they are a request-response shell wrapped around a much larger asynchronous engine. Order placed? Twelve services need to know about it, eventually, exactly once, in roughly the right order. That is not a synchronous problem.

Kafka is the dominant tool, so you'll learn Kafka specifically, but the patterns (event sourcing, CQRS, the outbox pattern, idempotent consumers, exactly-once semantics) transfer to anything async.

Kafka · Topics · Partitions · Consumer groups · Pub/sub · Event sourcing · CQRS · Outbox pattern · Idempotency · Exactly-once · Stream processing · Flink · Backpressure

05Operations

Reliability & ops

2 weeks/The part of system design no one practices

Most candidates can design a system. Almost none can answer "how would you know it's broken?" or "what happens when this region goes dark?" That is where senior interviews are won or lost.

This phase is short but disproportionately high-leverage. Two weeks of SRE principles will visibly change how you talk about every system in Phase 06.

SLO · SLI · SLA · Error budgets · Observability · Metrics · Logs · Traces · Rate limiting · Circuit breakers · Bulkheads · Graceful degradation · Capacity planning · Back-of-envelope math · Multi-region failover

06Application

Real architectures

4 weeks/Where everything compounds

You now know enough to design any system in the world. This phase is about practice. Twelve canonical problems, designed end-to-end on a blank canvas — no looking, no peeking, then compare against the reference.

The list: URL shortener · rate limiter · chat (WhatsApp) · news feed (Twitter) · ride sharing (Uber) · video streaming (YouTube) · distributed cache · payments · search (Google) · cloud storage (Dropbox) · ad serving · notification system. Do them in order. Spend two days per problem.

07Performance

Mock interviews

2 weeks/Knowing it isn't the same as performing it

The gap between "I know this material" and "I can perform it under interview pressure with a stranger nitpicking me" is enormous. Closing that gap is the entire purpose of this phase.

Do at least one mock per day for two weeks. Record yourself. Watch the recording — it will be excruciating, and that's how you improve. Vary the format: AI mocks for volume, peer mocks for realism, paid mocks with engineers for the final calibration.

Time management · Requirements clarification · Capacity estimation · Communicating trade-offs · Handling deep dives · Drawing under pressure · L5 vs L6 expectations · Senior & Staff signals

A few honest things, before you start.

The roadmap above is calibrated for ten hours per week of focused study. Less than that and you'll forget Phase 01 by the time you finish Phase 03. More and you'll halve the timeline. There is no shortcut.

  1. One primary resource per phase. Pick it, finish it, then move on. Reading the System Design Primer and watching ByteByteGo and reading DDIA in parallel is how people study for six months and learn nothing.
  2. Diagram on paper. Watching a video is passive. Redrawing the diagram from memory ten minutes later is what makes it stick. If you cannot redraw it, you do not understand it.
  3. Start mock interviews in Phase 02. Not Phase 07. You will be bad. That is the point. The gap between "knows it" and "can perform it" is its own skill, and it takes months — not weeks — to close.
  4. Pick one company's engineering blog and binge it. Ten posts deep from Netflix or Uber will teach you more about real architecture than a hundred surface-level YouTube videos.
  5. Quit DDIA if you must, but come back. Most engineers give up around Chapter 5. The chapter on replication is where the book opens up. Push through.

If you do this honestly, in six months you will not just pass system design interviews — you will enjoy them. That is the actual goal.
